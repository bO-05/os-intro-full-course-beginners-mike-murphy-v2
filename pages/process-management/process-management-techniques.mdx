# Process Management Techniques

Operating systems employ various process management techniques to ensure efficient and fair allocation of system resources among processes. These techniques include **scheduling algorithms** and **synchronization mechanisms**.

## Scheduling Algorithms

Scheduling algorithms determine the order in which processes are allocated CPU time. The goal is to maximize CPU utilization while minimizing the average waiting time and response time for processes. Some common scheduling algorithms include:

- First-Come, First-Served (FCFS)
- Shortest Job First (SJF)
- Priority Scheduling
- Round Robin (RR)
- Multi-level Queue Scheduling

Each algorithm has its own strengths and weaknesses, and the choice of algorithm depends on the specific requirements of the operating system and the types of processes it manages. For a detailed discussion on scheduling algorithms, refer to the [Scheduling Algorithms](/process-management/process-management-techniques/scheduling-algorithms) section.

## Synchronization

Synchronization is a crucial aspect of process management, as it ensures that processes can coordinate their activities and share resources without interfering with each other. Operating systems provide various synchronization primitives, such as:

- Mutex locks
- Semaphores
- Monitors
- Condition variables

These primitives help prevent race conditions, deadlocks, and other synchronization-related issues that can arise when multiple processes access shared resources concurrently. For a more in-depth look at synchronization, refer to the [Synchronization](/process-management/process-management-techniques/synchronization) section.
